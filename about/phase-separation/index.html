Phase separation in transcription

by Krishna Shrinivas

My PhD work has primarily focused on exploring and elucidating the role of phase separation in gene regulation, in collaboration with the Sharp and Young labs. Our studies were motivated by interesting observations and puzzles surrounding super enhancers (SEs), which were clusters of enhancer elements initially characterized by the Young lab.

  • SEs regulate genes important in cell-identity and disease
  • Occupied by unusually high densities of the transcription machinery
  • Highly sensitive to perturbation of bound factors
  • Close physical proximity despite spanning thousands of kilo-base pairs

Motivated by several studies that have shown a role for biomolecular condensation in diverse biological processes, we hypothesized that aspects of those puzzles could be explained through phase separation of the transcription machinery. Subsequently, in amazing experimental work led by our collaborators, we demonstrated that key transcription coactivators (BRD4 & MED1) form liquid-like condensates in vivo. The intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) of these proteins also phase separated in vitro.

To understand how transcription factors (TFs) impacted condensation, we turned to the activation domains (ADs) of TFs, which have been traditionally hard to crystallize due to their high disorder. We found that TFs often could phase separate on their own (at high concentrations) and partitioned with coactivators in vitro and in vivo, in an AD-dependent manner. Intruiguingly, the molecular features of TF-ADs that promote phase separation also promote gene activation in vivo in luciferase reporter assays.

More recently, we have begun to characterize the molecular features that lend specificity to this collective process - including aspects of the DNA (enhancer) code that drives spatio-temporally localized condensation. I am also exploring how non-equilibrium processes (ATP-driven processes) affect condensate properties, and thus, biological function.
