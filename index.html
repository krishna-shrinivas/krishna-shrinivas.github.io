Krishna Shrinivas
NSF-Simons Independent Research Fellow, Harvard University
I am currently an NSF-Simons Independent Fellow at Harvard . I am broadly interested in cellular physiology, with a focus on investigating the collective processes that underlie gene regulation, nuclear organization, and developmental processes. To this end, I develop models that integrate approaches from theory, simulation, and informatics, which are tested and refined in synergy with experimental collaborations.

I love working in teams - if you have an idea for a collaboration or just want to shoot the breeze about a cool observation, feel free to write me !
Areas of active interest include:
  • Biomolecular condensates in nuclear and genome organization
  • Specificity and robustness in biological systems
    (esp. problems of cooperativity, proofreading, evolvability)
During my PhD at the Chakraborty lab at MIT, I focused on studying the role of phase separation in gene regulation. In collaboration with the Sharp and Young labs, we hypothesized and demonstrated that the transcription machinery condenses into liquid-like droplets at particular genomic loci. More recently, we have begun to characterize the mechanisms underlying specificity and regulatory control, for example, aspects of the DNA (enhancer) code that drives spatio-temporally localized condensation and feedback regulation of transcriptional condensates mediated by active (i.e. non-equilibrium) RNA synthesis. Along the way, I have collaborated with other lab members on problems in cell signaling, evolutionary biology , and immunology.
